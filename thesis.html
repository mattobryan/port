Training that listens to the model it is attacking.

Adaptive Adversarial Training (AAT) is a curriculum for robust deep learning. Instead of attacking every class with the same fixed perturbation budget, AAT watches which classes and which threat norms the model is weakest against, then redirects attack strength and attack type toward those weaknesses, epoch by epoch. The result on CIFAR-10: union robustness rises from 40% to 49% against combined ℓ∞, ℓ2 and ℓ1 attacks, with clean accuracy improving rather than degrading.

The problem: one norm is never enough

A model hardened against ℓ∞ attacks (bounded per-pixel change) can still collapse under ℓ2 or ℓ1 perturbations, because each norm carves a differently shaped region around every input. Union robustness, accuracy under the worst of all three, is the metric that matters in deployment, and it is consistently the lowest. A second, quieter problem: classes are not equally fragile. Cats and birds break under perturbations that planes and trucks shrug off, yet standard adversarial training hands every class the same attack budget, overdefending the strong and abandoning the weak.

What is a perturbation, actually?

A perturbation is a tiny numeric nudge added to every pixel — one that's small enough to be invisible (or nearly so) to a human, but large enough to push the model's internal math past a decision boundary. 1 · Per-class ε budgets adapt to class robustness (ε, /255)
robust classes (ship, car) fragile classes (cat, dog, bird) static linear schedule (RAMP)

Update rule ε(c) ← ε(c) + α·(A(c) − τ). Classes that clear the target τ earn a harder attack; classes still struggling are held back so their decision margins can form. Lower τ and the whole curriculum turns aggressive.

One loop, two dials

1
Evaluate
After each epoch, measure robust accuracy per class A(c) and per norm R(p) on held-out data. This is the feedback signal everything else consumes.
2
Adapt
Raise ε for classes above the target τ, hold it for classes below. Reweight norm sampling toward whichever of ℓ∞, ℓ2, ℓ1 the model currently resists least.
3
Attack and train
Generate PGD adversarial examples under the sampled norms and adapted budgets, take the worst-case loss with a KL consistency term across norms, and update the weights.

The baseline this improves on, RAMP, pairs logits across two fixed norms with a fixed attack schedule. AAT keeps RAMP's pairing loss but replaces every static choice with a feedback-driven one, which turns the schedule itself into a curriculum: easy first, hard when earned, always pointed at the current weakness.

Adaptive ε scheduling delivers the largest single gain: +10 points on ℓ∞, +10 on ℓ1, +9 on union robustness, with clean accuracy up 2 points rather than traded away. Norm scheduling alone yields smaller, consistent gains; combined multi-norm variants (Ablation 3) extend the same feedback principle to attack selection. Evaluation via AutoAttack. Trained on a single 12 GB GPU with gradient accumulation, a deliberately low-resource setup.

Why this matters

Robustness research usually assumes clusters of A100s. This work shows that principled curriculum design, not raw compute, closes a meaningful share of the union-robustness gap on commodity hardware. That is the deployment reality for most of the world: models serving safety-relevant decisions in environments where retraining is expensive, attacks are unconstrained by any single norm, and the hardest classes are exactly the ones a uniform defense quietly abandons. AAT's contribution is a training procedure that allocates defensive effort the way an attacker allocates offensive effort: toward whatever is currently weakest.
